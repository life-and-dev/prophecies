# Thirty Pieces of Silver and the Potter

## The Prophecy

> Then I said to them, "If it seems good to you, give me my wages; but if not, keep them." And they weighed out as my wages thirty pieces of silver. Then the LORD said to me, "Throw it to the potter", the lordly price at which I was priced by them. So I took the thirty pieces of silver and threw them into the house of the LORD, to the potter. — Zechariah 11:12-13 (ESV)

## Fulfillment

### Apostles

> Then one of the twelve, whose name was Judas Iscariot, went to the chief priests and said, "What will you give me if I deliver him over to you?" And they paid him thirty pieces of silver. And from that moment he sought an opportunity to betray him. — Matthew 26:14-16 (ESV)

After Jesus' arrest and condemnation, Judas returned the money:

> Then when Judas, his betrayer, saw that Jesus was condemned, he changed his mind and brought back the thirty pieces of silver to the chief priests and the elders, saying, "I have sinned by betraying innocent blood." They said, "What is that to us? See to it yourself." And throwing down the pieces of silver into the temple, he departed, and he went and hanged himself. But the chief priests, taking the pieces of silver, said, "It is not lawful to put them into the treasury, since it is blood money." So they took counsel and bought with them the potter's field as a burial place for strangers. Therefore that field has been called the Field of Blood to this day. Then was fulfilled what had been spoken by the prophet Jeremiah, saying, "And they took the thirty pieces of silver, the price of him on whom a price had been set by some of the sons of Israel, and they gave them for the potter's field, as the Lord directed me." — Matthew 27:3-10 (ESV)

### Christian

Christians interpret this passage as a precise messianic prophecy fulfilled in the events of Jesus' betrayal, arguing that:

**Sequence of Specific Details:** The prophecy contains multiple specific details that match in an exact sequence:
1. **The Exact Amount:** Thirty pieces of silver. This was the contemptuous price of a slave according to the Law (Exodus 21:32: "If the ox gores a slave, male or female, the owner shall give to their master thirty shekels of silver"). This specific valuation demonstrates how cheaply Jesus was valued.
2. **Location:** Thrown into the house of the LORD (the temple).
3. **Use:** Connected to the potter (the purchase of the potter's field).

This precise combination of amount, location, and final use is considered too specific to be coincidental.

**Matthew's Explicit Recognition:** Matthew explicitly states that these events fulfilled prophecy. Although he attributes it to Jeremiah, likely referring to Jeremiah's visit to the potter in Jeremiah 18-19 or conflating prophetic themes, he clearly identifies the historical events as the intended fulfillment of the scriptural pattern.

**Type and Antitype Pattern:** Zechariah's symbolic action, where God's prophet received a slave's wage for his shepherding work, foreshadowed Jesus' betrayal. Just as God's care of Israel through His prophet was valued contemptuously, so God's Son was valued at the same amount. The "lordly price", a sarcastic reference to the low valuation, finds its ultimate irony in the rejection of the Messiah.

**Beyond Human Control:** Writing approximately 500 years before the events, Zechariah could not have fabricated these details based on the life of Jesus. The correspondence of multiple independent factors suggests prophetic foresight.

However, several contextual factors complicate this interpretation:

### Jews

**Not considered messianic.** Jewish scholars view Zechariah 11 as a symbolic prophetic action addressing the post-exilic period.

1. **Symbolic Prophetic Drama:** The passage presents a drama where Zechariah acts out God's relationship with Israel. The thirty pieces of silver represent the contemptuous payment Zechariah received for his work as a shepherd, symbolizing how little Israel valued God's care. This was a message for Zechariah's contemporaries, not a prediction of the distant future.
2. **Historical Context:** The context focuses on God's judgement upon Israel for rejecting His shepherding. The breaking of the staffs "Favour" and "Union" (Zechariah 11:10, 14) relates to historical realities of the post-exilic era, such as the division between Judah and Israel.
3. **No Messianic Markers:** The original Hebrew context contains no reference to the Messiah, a Davidic king, or future salvation. The shepherd imagery represents God or His prophet in that specific historical moment.
4. **Potter Reference Unclear:** The exact meaning of "throw it to the potter" is debated. Some scholars suggest "potter" (*yotzer*) may be a textual variant for "treasury" (*otzar*), or it might refer to a potter working at the temple. The connection to a literal field purchase 500 years later is seen as an interpretive layer added by the Gospel writers.

### Critical

Critical scholars view Zechariah 11:12-13 as an enactment of prophetic symbolism within Zechariah's ministry. They observe that the Gospel writers, particularly Matthew, were deeply immersed in the Hebrew Scriptures and sought to find meaningful patterns (midrashic interpretation) between the life of Jesus and earlier prophetic dramas. Matthew's attribution of the quote to "Jeremiah" (Matthew 27:9) instead of Zechariah suggests he may have been referencing a broader prophetic tradition or conflating different scrolls. From this perspective, the "fulfillment" is a creative theological application of earlier scripture to new events rather than a literal prediction.

## Conclusion

The correspondence of specific details: the exact amount of thirty pieces of silver, the location in the temple, and the connection to the potter is remarkable. Matthew explicitly cited these events as the fulfillment of a prophetic pattern. 

However, the original context of Zechariah 11:12-13 is a symbolic drama addressing the rejection of God's shepherding in the post-exilic period, with no explicit messianic markers. The interpretation depends on whether one views these parallels as intentional prophetic foreshadowing (type and antitype) or as a later theological application of symbolic language by the Gospel writers. Jewish scholars do not recognise this as a messianic prophecy, viewing it instead as a contemporary message to Israel.
