# He Will Command His Angels Concerning You

> **Fulfillment Category:** Misunderstood Context
>
> This psalm is written for anyone "dwelling in the shelter of the Most High" and promises protection and long life. Satan quoted this to tempt Jesus, but the promises (long life satisfaction, protection from danger) are incompatible with Jesus' brief earthly life and crucifixion. Jesus didn't need salvation as the psalm implies.

## The Prophecy

Psalm 91 is a psalm of divine protection:

> He who dwells in the shelter of the Most High will abide in the shadow of the Almighty. I will say to the LORD, "My refuge and my fortress, my God, in whom I trust."
>
> For he will deliver you from the snare of the fowler and from the deadly pestilence. He will cover you with his pinions, and under his wings you will find refuge; his faithfulness is a shield and buckler.
>
> You will not fear the terror of the night, nor the arrow that flies by day, nor the pestilence that stalks in darkness, nor the destruction that wastes at noonday... Because you have made the LORD your dwelling place—the Most High, who is my refuge—no evil shall be allowed to befall you, no plague come near your tent.
>
> **For he will command his angels concerning you to guard you in all your ways. On their hands they will bear you up, lest you strike your foot against a stone.**
>
> You will tread on the lion and the adder; the young lion and the serpent you will trample underfoot. "Because he holds fast to me in love, I will deliver him; I will protect him, because he knows my name. When he calls to me, I will answer him; I will be with him in trouble; I will rescue him and honor him. **With long life I will satisfy him and show him my salvation.**" — Psalm 91:1-16 (selected verses, ESV)

## Christian Interpretation

Satan quoted this psalm when tempting Jesus:

> Then the devil took him to the holy city and set him on the pinnacle of the temple and said to him, "If you are the Son of God, throw yourself down, for it is written, **'He will command his angels concerning you,' and 'On their hands they will bear you up, lest you strike your foot against a stone.'**" Jesus said to him, "Again it is written, 'You shall not put the Lord your God to the test.'" — Matthew 4:5-7 (ESV)

Some Christians interpret this as:
- **Messianic psalm** - Satan recognized it referred to the Messiah
- **Angels protecting Jesus** - God's supernatural protection of Jesus
- **Jesus' authority over evil** - Trampling serpents and lions

However, Jesus rejected Satan's application of this psalm.

## Original Context

### Why This Is Misunderstood

1. **The psalm is written for anyone who trusts God**: The opening says:
   > "**He who dwells** in the shelter of the Most High will abide in the shadow of the Almighty."

   This is a conditional promise for **anyone** who makes God their refuge, not exclusively for the Messiah. It's a general psalm of trust, not a messianic prophecy.

2. **Jesus rejected Satan's application**: When Satan quoted this psalm, Jesus didn't say "Yes, this is about me." Instead, he rebuked Satan for testing God. Jesus' response indicates he didn't view this psalm as specifically about himself.

3. **The psalm promises "long life"**: Verse 16 says:
   > "**With long life I will satisfy him** and show him my salvation."

   Jesus lived only about 33 years. Not a long life by ancient standards. People in that era could live 70-80 years or more (Psalm 90:10). Jesus' life was cut short by crucifixion, the opposite of this promise.

4. **The psalm promises protection from harm**: The psalm repeatedly promises:
   - Deliverance from snares and pestilence
   - Protection from arrows, terror, and destruction
   - "No evil shall be allowed to befall you"
   - "I will deliver him; I will protect him"
   - "I will rescue him and honor him"

   Jesus was:
   - Arrested, beaten, mocked
   - Crucified (evil befell him)
   - Not rescued from his enemies
   - Not protected from death

5. **Jesus didn't need salvation**: Verse 16 says, "show him my salvation." Jesus is the Savior—he doesn't need to be shown salvation. He came to provide salvation for others, not to receive it for himself.

6. **The conditional language applies to any believer**: The psalm uses second person ("you") and third person ("he who dwells"), indicating it's written for any godly person:
   - "You will not fear..."
   - "He will deliver you..."
   - "Because he holds fast to me in love..."

   This is wisdom literature offering encouragement to all who trust God, not a specific prophecy about one individual.

7. **The promises are idealized, not absolute**: Like many wisdom psalms, Psalm 91 presents an idealized picture of divine protection for the faithful. It's meant to encourage trust in God, not promise that no harm will ever come to believers. History shows many faithful people suffered (Job, the prophets, martyrs). The psalm is poetic encouragement, not a guarantee. And not a messianic prophecy.

8. **Satan's use proves it's not obviously messianic**: If this psalm were clearly about the Messiah, Satan's temptation would make no sense. Satan was testing whether Jesus would presume upon God's protection. The fact that this was presented as a test shows the psalm wasn't understood as exclusively messianic—it was a general promise Satan tried to get Jesus to misapply.

### Assessment

Psalm 91 is a wisdom psalm offering encouragement and assurance to anyone who trusts in God. It promises divine protection and long life to the faithful. The psalm is written in general terms ("he who dwells," "you will not fear") applicable to any believer, not exclusively to one person.

When Satan quoted this psalm to tempt Jesus, Jesus rejected the application. The psalm's promises—long life, protection from harm, deliverance from danger, being shown salvation—don't match Jesus' experience. He lived a brief life, was not protected from crucifixion, and didn't need salvation for himself.

The Christian interpretation depends on:
- Ignoring that the psalm is addressed to anyone who trusts God ("he who dwells")
- Overlooking Jesus' rejection of Satan's application
- Disregarding the promise of "long life" that contradicts Jesus' brief earthly existence
- Ignoring that Jesus was not protected from harm as the psalm promises
- Treating Satan's misuse of Scripture during temptation as validation that it's messianic
- Overlooking that Jesus didn't need to be "shown salvation"

**Strength of Fulfillment**: Misunderstood Context. The passage is a general wisdom psalm for anyone who trusts God, promising protection and long life—promises incompatible with Jesus' brief life and crucifixion.
