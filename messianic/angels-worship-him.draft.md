# Let All God's Angels Worship Him

> **Fulfillment Category:** Misunderstood Context
>
> The psalm explicitly addresses worshiping YHWH (the LORD/God the Father), not a separate messianic figure. Hebrews applies this to the Son, but Jesus himself taught "worship the LORD your God and Him only" (Matthew 4:10), directing worship to the Father, not himself.

## The Prophecy

Psalm 97 is a hymn celebrating God's reign:

> The LORD reigns, let the earth rejoice; let the many coastlands be glad! Clouds and thick darkness are all around him; righteousness and justice are the foundation of his throne. Fire goes before him and burns up his adversaries all around.
>
> His lightnings light up the world; the earth sees and trembles. The mountains melt like wax before the LORD, before the Lord of all the earth. The heavens proclaim his righteousness, and all the peoples see his glory.
>
> All worshipers of images are put to shame, who make their boast in worthless idols; **worship him, all you gods!** Zion hears and is glad, and the daughters of Judah rejoice, because of your judgments, O LORD.
>
> For you, O LORD, are most high over all the earth; you are exalted far above all gods. — Psalm 97:1-9 (selected verses, ESV)

## Christian Interpretation

The book of Hebrews quotes this psalm and applies it to Jesus:

> And again, when he brings the firstborn into the world, he says, **"Let all God's angels worship him."** — Hebrews 1:6 (ESV)

Christians interpret this as:
- **Angels worship the Son** - Divine status of Jesus
- **"Firstborn"** - Jesus as preeminent
- **Worship due to deity** - Jesus is worthy of worship like the Father

The psalm is understood as prophesying that angels would worship the Messiah.

## Original Context

### Why This Is Misunderstood

1. **The entire psalm addresses YHWH (the LORD)**: Throughout the psalm:
   - "**The LORD** reigns" (v. 1)
   - "Before **the LORD**, before **the Lord of all the earth**" (v. 5)
   - "Because of your judgments, **O LORD**" (v. 8)
   - "For you, **O LORD**, are most high over all the earth" (v. 9)

   The psalm consistently speaks about YHWH (God the Father), not a separate messianic figure.

2. **"Worship him, all you gods" refers to YHWH**: In context, the command "worship him, all you gods" (v. 7) follows immediately after describing YHWH's glory and power. The "him" refers to "the LORD" mentioned throughout the psalm. Ancient translations render "gods" (*elohim*) as "angels," but either way, they're commanded to worship YHWH.

3. **Jesus taught worship belongs to God alone**: When tempted by Satan, Jesus said:
   > "Be gone, Satan! For it is written, '**You shall worship the Lord your God and him only shall you serve**.'" — Matthew 4:10 (ESV)

   Jesus directed worship to "the Lord your God"—the Father. He didn't claim worship for himself but redirected it to the Father.

4. **Jesus consistently pointed people to the Father**: Throughout his ministry, Jesus:
   - Prayed to the Father (Matthew 6:9)
   - Said "the Father is greater than I" (John 14:28)
   - Directed glory to the Father (John 17:1-5)
   - Called the Father "my God" even after resurrection (John 20:17)

   Jesus' pattern was directing worship and honor to the Father, not claiming it for himself in the way this psalm describes.

5. **Hebrews interprets, not quotes directly**: Hebrews 1:6 doesn't claim Psalm 97 originally prophesied the Messiah. The book of Hebrews uses a midrashic interpretive method, applying Old Testament texts about God to Christ to argue for Christ's deity. This is theological argument, not claiming the psalm's original intent was messianic prophecy.

6. **The psalm celebrates God's sovereignty over all**: The theme is YHWH's supreme reign:
   - Mountains melt before him (v. 5)
   - The earth trembles (v. 4)
   - He is "exalted far above all gods" (v. 9)
   - Fire goes before him burning his adversaries (v. 3)

   This is cosmic theophany—God revealing his glory. It's not describing the Messiah but celebrating God's own supreme majesty.

7. **Monotheistic context**: For the original Jewish audience, this psalm celebrated the one true God (YHWH) in contrast to pagan idols and false gods. The command for angels/gods to worship him emphasizes YHWH's uniqueness and supremacy. Reading this as worship of a second divine person (the Son) wasn't the original intent in a strictly monotheistic context.

### Assessment

Psalm 97 is a hymn celebrating YHWH's (the LORD's) sovereign reign over all creation. It calls all beings, including angels ("gods"), to worship YHWH because of his supreme majesty and power. The psalm consistently identifies its subject as "the LORD" (YHWH) throughout.

Hebrews applies this to Jesus as part of its theological argument for Christ's deity, using a midrashic interpretive method. However, the original psalm doesn't distinguish between Father and Son—it celebrates the one God of Israel.

The Christian interpretation depends on:
- Extracting one phrase while ignoring that the entire psalm addresses "the LORD" (YHWH)
- Reading Trinitarian theology (Father and Son as distinct persons) into a monotheistic psalm
- Overlooking that Jesus taught worship belongs to "the Lord your God" (the Father)
- Treating Hebrews' midrashic application as equivalent to original prophetic intent
- Ignoring the psalm's consistent focus on YHWH's unique sovereignty

**Strength of Fulfillment**: Misunderstood Context. The passage celebrates YHWH's (the LORD's) reign and calls all beings to worship him. The original context is strictly monotheistic, celebrating the one God of Israel, not prophesying worship of a separate messianic figure.
