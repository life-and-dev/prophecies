# May Another Take His Office

> **Fulfillment Category:** Misunderstood Context
>
> This is David's imprecatory prayer requesting God to curse his wicked accuser, including harsh judgments on the accuser's family. Jesus never cursed anyone this way or requested divine judgment. Peter applied a general principle to Judas's replacement, not acknowledging prophecy about Jesus.

## The Prophecy

Psalm 109 is David's imprecatory prayer against his enemies:

> O God of my praise! Do not be silent! For wicked and deceitful mouths are opened against me, speaking against me with lying tongues. They encircle me with words of hate, and attack me without cause. In return for my love they accuse me, but I give myself to prayer. So they reward me evil for good, and hatred for my love.
>
> Appoint a wicked man against him; let an accuser stand at his right hand. When he is tried, let him come forth guilty; let his prayer be counted as sin! **May his days be few; may another take his office!** May his children be fatherless and his wife a widow! May his children wander about and beg, seeking food far from the ruins they inhabit!
>
> May the creditor seize all that he has; may strangers plunder the fruits of his toil! Let there be none to extend kindness to him, nor any to pity his fatherless children!... Let the iniquity of his fathers be remembered before the LORD, and let not the sin of his mother be blotted out!
>
> For he did not remember to show kindness, but pursued the poor and needy and the brokenhearted, to put them to death. He loved to curse; let curses come upon him! He did not delight in blessing; may it be far from him! — Psalm 109:1-17 (selected verses, ESV)

## Christian Interpretation

Peter quoted this psalm when the disciples chose Judas's replacement:

> "For it is written in the Book of Psalms, 'May his camp become desolate, and let there be no one to dwell in it'; and **'Let another take his office.'** So one of the men who have accompanied us during all the time that the Lord Jesus went in and out among us... must become with us a witness to his resurrection." — Acts 1:20-22 (ESV)

Christians interpret this as:
- **Prophesying Judas's betrayal** - The wicked man who needed to be replaced
- **Apostolic succession** - Another taking Judas's office (apostleship)
- **Divine judgment on Judas** - The curses pronounced falling on Judas

The psalm is understood as prophesying Judas's betrayal and replacement.

## Original Context

### Why This Is Misunderstood

1. **This is David's personal imprecatory prayer**: The superscription reads "To the choirmaster. A Psalm of David." This is David's prayer against his enemies:
   - People who "speak against me with lying tongues" (v. 2)
   - Those who "reward me evil for good" (v. 5)
   - His personal accusers who attack him "without cause" (v. 3)

   This is about David's actual historical enemies, not a prophecy about future events.

2. **The entire psalm is a curse prayer**: David requests God to:
   - Make his accuser's children fatherless and his wife a widow (v. 9)
   - Make his children wander and beg (v. 10)
   - Seize all his possessions (v. 11)
   - Remember the sins of his parents (v. 14)
   - Let curses come upon him (v. 17-18)

   These are harsh imprecations (curse prayers) asking God to judge his enemies. This was David's form of justice-seeking, not messianic prophecy.

3. **Jesus never prayed curses on anyone**: Throughout his ministry, Jesus:
   - Taught "love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you" (Matthew 5:44)
   - Said "Father, forgive them, for they know not what they do" while being crucified (Luke 23:34)
   - Rebuked disciples who wanted to call down fire on a Samaritan village (Luke 9:54-55)

   Jesus' entire approach contradicts the vengeful spirit of Psalm 109. He never requested that his betrayer's children become fatherless or that his wife become a widow.

4. **Peter applied a general principle, not a specific prophecy**: Peter was facing a practical problem: Judas had died, leaving a vacancy among the Twelve. Peter found Scriptures that dealt with:
   - A wicked person's office becoming vacant
   - Another taking that position

   This is applying a general principle from Scripture to a current situation, not claiming David prophesied Judas specifically.

5. **"Office" (pekudah) just means "position" or "oversight"**: The Hebrew word doesn't specifically mean "apostleship." It's a general term for any position of responsibility or oversight. In Psalm 109, it likely refers to some civic or military position held by David's enemy.

6. **The psalm describes someone "pursuing the poor and needy... to put them to death"**: Verse 16 says the wicked man "pursued the poor and needy and the brokenhearted, to put them to death." This describes:
   - Active persecution of multiple people
   - A pattern of violence against the vulnerable
   - Intentional murder

   Judas betrayed Jesus, but he didn't fit this description of systematically pursuing and murdering the poor and needy.

7. **David seeks vengeance; Jesus forbade it**: The entire tone of Psalm 109 is vengeance:
   - "Let curses come upon him!" (v. 17)
   - "Clothe him with his curses like a coat" (v. 18-19)
   - "May this be the reward of my accusers from the LORD" (v. 20)

   Jesus taught "Do not resist the one who is evil" (Matthew 5:39) and "Vengeance is mine, I will repay, says the Lord" (Romans 12:19). The psalm's spirit contradicts Jesus' teaching.

### Assessment

Psalm 109 is David's intense imprecatory prayer asking God to judge his enemies—people who attacked him with lies and repaid his love with hatred. The harsh curses (children becoming orphans, wife a widow, possessions seized, family line cursed) reflect ancient Near Eastern justice concepts and David's cry for vindication.

Peter applied one phrase ("may another take his office") to the practical need to replace Judas among the Twelve. This is a hermeneutical principle application—using Scripture's wisdom for current situations—not claiming David prophesied Judas's betrayal 1,000 years in advance.

The Christian interpretation depends on:
- Extracting one phrase from a lengthy curse prayer
- Ignoring that this contradicts Jesus' teaching on loving enemies
- Overlooking that Jesus never prayed such curses on Judas or anyone
- Treating Peter's practical application as equivalent to claiming direct prophecy
- Disregarding that the psalm's description of the wicked man doesn't fit Judas
- Assuming any Scripture applied by apostles must have been originally prophetic

**Strength of Fulfillment**: Misunderstood Context. The passage is David's curse prayer against his personal enemies, requesting harsh judgments completely contrary to Jesus' teaching and practice. Peter applied one principle to select Judas's replacement, not claiming direct prophecy.
