# Dominion Over Creation

> **Fulfillment Category:** Misunderstood Context
>
> The original text addresses humans generally, not exclusively Jesus. Christians apply it to Jesus through selective interpretation, but the psalm uses plurals and describes attributes common to all humanity.

## The Prophecy

Psalm 8 celebrates humanity's honored position in creation:

> O LORD, our Lord, how majestic is your name in all the earth! You have set your glory above the heavens. Out of the mouth of **babes and infants**, you have established strength because of your foes, to still the enemy and the avenger.
>
> When I look at your heavens, the work of your fingers, the moon and the stars, which you have set in place, what is man that you are mindful of him, and the son of man that you care for him?
>
> Yet you have made him a little lower than the heavenly beings and crowned him with glory and honor. **You have given him dominion over the works of your hands; you have put all things under his feet**, all sheep and oxen, and also the beasts of the field, the birds of the heavens, and the fish of the sea, whatever passes along the paths of the seas. — Psalm 8:1-8 (ESV)

## Christian Interpretation

The book of Hebrews quotes this passage and applies it to Jesus:

> For it was not to angels that God subjected the world to come, of which we are speaking. It has been testified somewhere, "What is man, that you are mindful of him, or the son of man, that you care for him? You made him for a little while lower than the angels; you have crowned him with glory and honor, **putting everything in subjection under his feet.**"
>
> Now in putting everything in subjection to him, he left nothing outside his control. At present, we do not yet see everything in subjection to him. But we see him who for a little while was made lower than the angels, namely Jesus, crowned with glory and honor because of the suffering of death. — Hebrews 2:5-9 (ESV)

Christians interpret this as:
- **"Son of man"** - Jesus' self-designation
- **"Made lower than angels"** - Jesus' incarnation and humiliation
- **"Crowned with glory"** - Jesus' resurrection and ascension
- **"All things under his feet"** - Jesus' ultimate authority over creation

The passage is understood as prophesying Jesus's authority and dominion over all creation.

## Original Context

### Why This Is Misunderstood

1. **The passage uses plurals**: The psalm refers to "**babes and infants**" (plural), suggesting it addresses humans generally, not exclusively one person (Jesus). This is a psalm about humanity's place in creation.

2. **Universal human experience**: The psalm describes attributes that apply to all humans, not uniquely to Jesus. Many humans exercise dominion over animals and nature—farming, animal husbandry, fishing, and environmental stewardship are universal human activities.

3. **"Son of man" is not a title here**: In Hebrew poetry, "son of man" (ben adam) is synonymous parallelism with "man" (enosh)—a poetic device meaning simply "human being." It's not a messianic title in this context.

4. **David's perspective**: The psalm is attributed to David and expresses wonder at God's care for humanity despite our smallness compared to the vastness of creation. It's a meditation on the dignity God granted to all humans in Genesis 1:26-28.

5. **Genesis creation account**: The psalm clearly echoes Genesis 1:26-28 where God gave humanity (collectively) dominion over creation:
   > Then God said, "Let us make man in our image, after our likeness. And let them have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over the livestock and over all the earth..."

6. **Nothing unique to Jesus**: Every element mentioned—being made lower than angels, having dominion over animals, being crowned with honor—applies to humans generally according to the creation narrative. The psalm celebrates what God gave to all humanity, not a prophecy about one specific person.

### Assessment

The psalm is a beautiful meditation on human dignity and God's care for humanity. It celebrates the creation mandate given to all humans in Genesis. While Christians can legitimately apply the psalm's themes to Jesus as the perfect human and second Adam, the original context clearly addresses humanity collectively, not a specific messianic prophecy.

The Christian interpretation depends on:
- Isolating "son of man" as a title rather than reading it as Hebrew parallelism for "human"
- Ignoring the plural references to "babes and infants"
- Overlooking that dominion over animals was given to all humanity in Genesis
- Applying typology (Jesus as ideal human) rather than recognizing direct prophecy

**Strength of Fulfillment**: Misunderstood Context. The passage is about humanity's place in creation, not a specific prophecy about the Messiah.
