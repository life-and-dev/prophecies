# The King of Glory Entering Gates

> **Fulfillment Category:** Misunderstood Context
>
> This psalm celebrates God (YHWH) as the King of glory entering Jerusalem, likely composed for bringing the Ark of the Covenant into the city. The "everlasting doors" suggest symbolic or heavenly gates, not the literal gates of historical Jerusalem.

## The Prophecy

Psalm 24 is a processional psalm celebrating God's entrance:

> The earth is the LORD's and the fullness thereof, the world and those who dwell therein, for he has founded it upon the seas and established it upon the rivers.
>
> Who shall ascend the hill of the LORD? And who shall stand in his holy place? He who has clean hands and a pure heart, who does not lift up his soul to what is false and does not swear deceitfully.
>
> **Lift up your heads, O gates! And be lifted up, O ancient doors, that the King of glory may come in. Who is this King of glory? The LORD, strong and mighty, the LORD, mighty in battle!**
>
> Lift up your heads, O gates! And lift them up, O ancient doors, that the King of glory may come in. **Who is this King of glory? The LORD of hosts, he is the King of glory!** — Psalm 24:1-10 (ESV)

## Christian Interpretation

Christians interpret this psalm as referring to Jesus because:
- **"King of glory"** - Jesus entering Jerusalem or ascending to heaven
- **"Lift up your heads, O gates"** - Jesus' triumphal entry into Jerusalem
- **"Ancient doors"** - Gates of heaven opening for Jesus' ascension
- **"Mighty in battle"** - Jesus' victory over sin and death
- **"LORD of hosts"** - Jesus as divine King

The psalm is understood as prophesying either Jesus' triumphal entry into Jerusalem or his ascension into heaven.

## Original Context

### Why This Is Misunderstood

1. **The psalm explicitly identifies the King of glory as YHWH (the LORD)**: The text answers its own question twice:
   - "Who is this King of glory? **The LORD**, strong and mighty"
   - "Who is this King of glory? **The LORD of hosts**"

   This is about God (YHWH), not about a separate messianic figure. If Christians interpret this as Jesus, they must argue Jesus is YHWH—which creates theological complexity.

2. **"Everlasting doors" don't match Jerusalem's literal gates**: The phrase "ancient doors" or "everlasting doors" (Hebrew: *pitchei olam*) suggests eternal, timeless gates. Jerusalem's literal city gates were:
   - Built in historical time
   - Destroyed and rebuilt multiple times
   - Not "everlasting" in any sense

   The language is poetic and symbolic, not describing a literal historical event.

3. **Historical context: Bringing the Ark to Jerusalem**: Many scholars believe this psalm was composed for when David brought the Ark of the Covenant to Jerusalem (2 Samuel 6). The Ark represented God's presence, so the psalm celebrates God's entrance into his city. This was a specific historical event, not a prophecy.

4. **Liturgical processional psalm**: The structure suggests this was used in worship processions:
   - First section: hymn to God as creator (vv. 1-2)
   - Second section: entrance liturgy asking who may enter God's presence (vv. 3-6)
   - Third section: antiphonal call for gates to open for God (vv. 7-10)

   The call-and-response format indicates liturgical use, with priests or Levites calling from outside while others respond from inside.

5. **Jesus' entry into Jerusalem doesn't match**: When Jesus entered Jerusalem:
   - He rode a donkey (Zechariah 9:9)
   - People shouted "Hosanna" and spread cloaks (Matthew 21:1-11)
   - No mention of gates lifting up or ancient doors opening
   - He was rejected by Jerusalem's leaders, not welcomed as "King of glory"
   - He wept over Jerusalem (Luke 19:41)

   The triumphal entry narrative doesn't contain the imagery of Psalm 24.

6. **The psalm emphasizes God's sovereignty over creation**: The opening verses establish that "the earth is the LORD's" because he founded and established it. This is about the creator God entering his own sanctuary, not about a human Messiah entering a city.

7. **"LORD of hosts" is a divine title**: The title *YHWH Sabaoth* (LORD of hosts/armies) refers to God commanding the heavenly armies. It's a title of divine sovereignty, used throughout the Old Testament for God himself, not for a human messiah or even the Messiah as a separate entity.

### Assessment

Psalm 24 is a liturgical processional celebrating God (YHWH) entering Jerusalem, likely when the Ark of the Covenant was brought to the city. The psalm explicitly identifies the "King of glory" as "the LORD" and "the LORD of hosts"—divine titles for God himself. The language of "everlasting doors" is poetic and symbolic, not describing literal historical gates.

The Christian interpretation depends on:
- Identifying Jesus with YHWH (the LORD of hosts)
- Reading the poetic, symbolic language as literal prophecy
- Ignoring that the psalm was likely written about a historical event (bringing the Ark)
- Imposing the triumphal entry narrative onto a psalm that doesn't contain those details
- Assuming liturgical worship psalms must be prophetic rather than recognizing their original ritual function

**Strength of Fulfillment**: Misunderstood Context. The passage celebrates God (YHWH) entering Jerusalem, using poetic liturgical language for a worship procession, not prophesying a future Messiah.
