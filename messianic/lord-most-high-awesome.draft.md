# The LORD Most High Is Awesome

> **Fulfillment Category:** Misunderstood Context
>
> This psalm celebrates God (YHWH) as the great King over all the earth. The psalm addresses God being exalted, not someone else exalting God. A monotheistic nation wouldn't understand one God exalting another—"exalted" means elevated in importance among earthly powers, not divine ascension.

## The Prophecy

Psalm 47 is a hymn celebrating God's kingship:

> Clap your hands, all peoples! Shout to God with loud songs of joy! For **the LORD, the Most High, is awesome, a great King over all the earth.** He subdued peoples under us, and nations under our feet. He chose our heritage for us, the pride of Jacob whom he loves.
>
> **God has gone up with a shout, the LORD with the sound of a trumpet.** Sing praises to God, sing praises! Sing praises to our King, sing praises!
>
> For God is the King of all the earth; sing praises with a psalm! **God reigns over the nations; God sits on his holy throne.** The princes of the peoples gather as the people of the God of Abraham. For the shields of the earth belong to God; **he is highly exalted!** — Psalm 47:1-9 (ESV)

## Christian Interpretation

Christians sometimes interpret this psalm as referring to Jesus because:
- **"God has gone up"** - Jesus' ascension to heaven
- **"With the sound of a trumpet"** - Triumphant ascension or second coming
- **"King over all the earth"** - Jesus' ultimate reign
- **"Sits on his holy throne"** - Jesus seated at God's right hand
- **"Highly exalted"** - Jesus' exaltation after resurrection

The psalm is understood as prophesying the Messiah's ascension and enthronement.

## Original Context

### Why This Is Misunderstood

1. **The psalm explicitly addresses YHWH (the LORD)**: Throughout the psalm, "the LORD" (YHWH), "God" (Elohim), and "Most High" (Elyon) all refer to God the Father, not a separate messianic figure. The psalm celebrates God himself, not someone else.

2. **God doesn't exalt himself**: The psalm says "**he is highly exalted**" (v. 9). In Christian theology, God the Father exalted Jesus (Philippians 2:9; Acts 2:33). But God the Father doesn't exalt himself—he is inherently supreme. The psalm's statement makes sense for a human king or for God's reputation being elevated among the nations, not for God exalting himself.

3. **Monotheistic Jews wouldn't understand God exalting God**: A Jewish audience reading "the LORD Most High" being "highly exalted" would not think of one person of a Trinity exalting another. They understood YHWH as one indivisible God. The language means God's reputation and recognition among nations is elevated, not divine ascension.

4. **"God has gone up" refers to procession to the temple**: Verse 5 says "God has gone up with a shout, the LORD with the sound of a trumpet." This likely refers to:
   - Liturgical processions where the Ark of the Covenant (representing God's presence) was carried up to the temple
   - Celebration of God's enthronement in worship
   - Symbolic language of God dwelling on Mount Zion (Jerusalem)

   This is temple worship imagery, not prophecy of physical ascension.

5. **"Exalted" means increased recognition among nations**: The psalm celebrates how God subdued nations under Israel (v. 3) and how "the princes of the peoples gather as the people of the God of Abraham" (v. 9). God's "exaltation" means:
   - His reputation spreading among gentile nations
   - Nations recognizing his sovereignty
   - His fame and glory increasing

   This is about God's glory being acknowledged, not about ascension to a higher position.

6. **The psalm is past tense celebrating historical victories**: "He subdued peoples under us, and nations under our feet" (v. 3). This celebrates Israel's historical military victories over surrounding nations, attributing them to God's power. It's thanksgiving for what God already accomplished, not future prophecy.

7. **Context is corporate worship**: The psalm calls "all peoples" to "clap your hands" and "shout to God with loud songs of joy" (v. 1). Commands like "Sing praises to God, sing praises!" (v. 6) show this is a liturgical hymn for communal worship, celebrating God's ongoing reign, not prophesying a future event.

### Assessment

Psalm 47 is a hymn celebrating God (YHWH) as King over all the earth. It calls all peoples to praise God for his sovereignty and for giving Israel victory over nations. The language of God being "exalted" and "going up" refers to:
- God's glory and recognition increasing among nations
- Liturgical temple processions
- God's symbolic enthronement in worship

The psalm addresses God himself throughout, not a separate messianic figure. In monotheistic Jewish understanding, God doesn't exalt himself—he is already supreme. The "exaltation" means his reputation and acknowledgment growing among the nations.

The Christian interpretation depends on:
- Reading "God has gone up" as physical ascension rather than liturgical procession
- Interpreting "highly exalted" as God exalting God rather than God's glory increasing among nations
- Ignoring that the psalm consistently addresses YHWH directly
- Imposing Trinitarian theology (Father exalting Son) on a text written for monotheistic worship
- Treating a hymn about God's present reign as prophecy about Jesus' ascension

**Strength of Fulfillment**: Misunderstood Context. The passage is a hymn celebrating God's (YHWH's) sovereignty and reign, using liturgical and symbolic language about his glory among nations, not prophesying the Messiah's ascension.
