# The LORD Saves His Anointed

> **Fulfillment Category:** Misunderstood Context
>
> This psalm is a prayer for the king going into battle. David was also anointed and a king, and the psalm's promises don't align with Jesus' crucifixion—God did not protect Jesus from death as this psalm promises.

## The Prophecy

Psalm 20 is a prayer for the king's victory in battle:

> May the LORD answer you in the day of trouble! May the name of the God of Jacob protect you! May he send you help from the sanctuary and give you support from Zion!
>
> May he remember all your offerings and regard with favor your burnt sacrifices! May he grant you your heart's desire and fulfill all your plans! May we shout for joy over your salvation, and in the name of our God set up our banners!
>
> **Now I know that the LORD saves his anointed; he will answer him from his holy heaven with the saving might of his right hand.** Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the LORD our God.
>
> They collapse and fall, but we rise and stand upright. O LORD, save the king! May he answer us when we call. — Psalm 20:1-9 (ESV)

## Christian Interpretation

Christians interpret this psalm as referring to Jesus because:
- **"His anointed"** - The Messiah (Christ means "anointed one")
- **"The LORD saves"** - God's deliverance through Jesus
- **"Answer him from his holy heaven"** - Jesus' resurrection and ascension
- **"We rise and stand upright"** - Resurrection victory
- **"Save the king"** - Jesus as the messianic King

The psalm is understood as prophesying God's deliverance of the Messiah and victory over enemies.

## Original Context

### Why This Is Misunderstood

1. **The superscription identifies this as David's psalm**: The heading reads "To the choirmaster. A Psalm of David." This is a liturgical prayer for the king, most likely composed for use before military campaigns.

2. **David was also anointed**: David was anointed as king by Samuel (1 Samuel 16:13). Throughout the Old Testament, "the LORD's anointed" referred to Israel's reigning king, not exclusively to a future Messiah. When this psalm says "his anointed," it refers to David or whoever was the current king of Israel.

3. **The psalm requests protection from danger**: The prayer asks God to:
   - Answer in the day of trouble
   - Send help and support
   - Remember offerings and sacrifices
   - Grant heart's desire
   - Save from enemies

   These are requests for military victory and protection from death in battle.

4. **God did not protect Jesus this way**: Unlike the psalm's promises:
   - Jesus was not protected in his "day of trouble" (Gethsemane, trial, crucifixion)
   - God did not send help to prevent his death
   - Jesus' prayer "let this cup pass from me" was not granted in the way requested
   - Jesus was captured, tried, and executed—not saved from his enemies

5. **The psalm promises victory over enemies who "collapse and fall"**: The enemies collapse while "we rise and stand upright." This describes military victory where the king's army defeats their foes on the battlefield. It doesn't describe resurrection after death.

6. **Context is corporate worship before battle**: Verses like "may we shout for joy" and "we trust in the name of the LORD" show this is a communal prayer, likely recited before the king went to war. It's a liturgical piece, not a prophecy.

7. **The language is present tense, not future**: "Now I know that the LORD saves his anointed"—this is a statement of confidence in God's current protection of the current king, not a prophecy about a future Messiah.

### Assessment

Psalm 20 is a prayer for Israel's king going into battle, asking God for military victory and protection from enemies. The "anointed" is the reigning king (David or his successors), not a future Messiah. The psalm's promises of protection and deliverance directly contradict what happened to Jesus, who was not saved from his enemies but was crucified.

The Christian interpretation depends on:
- Ignoring that "anointed" was a standard title for Israel's kings
- Reading "saves" as resurrection rather than protection from death
- Overlooking that God did not grant Jesus the kind of deliverance described in this psalm
- Applying a liturgical prayer for current kings to a future Messiah without textual warrant

**Strength of Fulfillment**: Misunderstood Context. The passage is a prayer for Israel's king in battle, not a prophecy about the Messiah, and its promises contradict Jesus' actual experience.
