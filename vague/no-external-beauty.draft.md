# No External Beauty

## The Prophecy

> Who has believed what he has heard from us? And to whom has the arm of the LORD been revealed? For he grew up before him like a young plant, and like a root out of dry ground; **he had no form or majesty that we should look at him, and no beauty that we should desire him**. — Isaiah 53:1-2 (ESV)

## Fulfillment

Isaiah's prophecy is often misunderstood in the western world as if Isaiah prophesied that Jesus would look ugly. However, the Hebrew mindset focuses less on outward appearance and more on what one does. In this case Isaiah prophesied not to expect a royal prince in a beautiful palace to proclaim the Messiah title.

Jesus grew up in humble circumstances, not in a palace. He was a carpenter's son from Nazareth, a town so insignificant it provoked the question: *"Can anything good come out of Nazareth?"*

In addition, Jesus had:

- **No political power**: Born in stable, grew up in obscurity
- **Rejected by own people** (John 1:11): "His own people did not receive him"

## Jewish Perspective

*See "Jewish Perspective on Isaiah 52-53 (Overview)" in the main prophecies document—servant interpreted as Israel collectively.*

Although the [Hebrew text and traditional Jewish commentaries](https://www.sefaria.org/Isaiah.53) disagree with Christians on the definition of "servant", they do confirm that the description of the "servant" means he is *one lacking outward grandeur*.

## Christian Perspective

The prophecy accurately describes Jesus' earthly ministry—no royal trappings, no political power, no impressive credentials that would attract followers based on external appearance. His authority was spiritual, not worldly.

Jesus perfectly fulfills the description of growing up without external majesty or beauty that would draw people to him based on appearances.

## Conclusion

Although Jesus completely fulfills the prophecy, there were many other great "servants" in history who also did significant work for God in humble circumstances. This prophecy alone is not enough to prove that Jesus is the Messiah and should be considered together with the wider body of evidence.

For example:

- David — a shepherd anointed by Samuel before he was publicly known (1 Samuel 16:11-13); his shepherd background is emphasized (1 Samuel 17:34-37).
- Amos — explicitly describes himself as not being a professional prophet but a shepherd and a dresser of sycamore trees (Amos 7:14-15).
- The Twelve (including fishermen like Peter and Andrew) — ordinary working men called from their nets to follow Jesus (Matthew 4:18-22).
- John the Baptist — lived and ministered in the wilderness, wore rough clothing, and had no courtly standing (Matthew 3:4; Mark 1:6; Luke 1:80).
